Extra Notes

Two replication cycles, three origin theories, and the difference between drift and shift.

Lytic vs Lysogenic Cycles

  • Lytic: the phage injects DNA, hijacks the cell, builds new phages, then produces endolysin — an enzyme that punches a hole in the bacterium. The cell bursts and dies, releasing the new viruses.
  • Lysogenic: the phage integrates its DNA into the bacterial genome and goes dormant. Every time the bacterium divides, the viral DNA is copied along with it. One day it re-awakens and switches into the lytic cycle.

Three Origin Theories

  • Virus first: viruses existed before the first cells. They were the original self-replicating molecules from which cellular life later arose.
  • Regressive evolution: viruses are descendants of free-living cells that lost most of their genes and machinery, becoming obligate parasites.
  • Escape hypothesis: viruses are bits of genetic material (DNA or RNA) that escaped from cellular hosts and learned to replicate independently.

Antigenic Drift vs Shift

  • Drift: small, gradual mutations in the virus's surface proteins. This is why flu vaccines need to be updated every year.
  • Shift: a sudden, large change — usually when two virus strains swap whole gene segments (reassortment) inside the same host. Can create a brand-new strain that humans have no immunity to, triggering pandemics.
