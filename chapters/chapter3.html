Extra Notes

The 2×2 of immunity, three vaccine types, and a Hungarian story you should know.

Active vs Passive · Natural vs Artificial

Two axes. Active means your body builds its own memory cells. Passive means you receive ready-made antibodies. Natural means it happens through ordinary life; artificial means a medical intervention.

NaturalArtificial
ActiveCatching a disease and surviving — you build memory cells.A vaccine — your body makes memory cells without the real illness.
PassiveMother's antibodies across the placenta and in breast milk.Antibody injection (e.g. antivenom, rabies immunoglobulin).

Three Types of Vaccines

  • Killed / Inactivated: invaders that can't do harm — the germ is killed before injection. Easy for the immune system to deal with.
  • Subunit (ripped to pieces): only certain parts of the germ are injected. Safe, but a weaker stimulus.
  • Live attenuated: a weakened "cousin" of the real germ, bred in the lab. Strong response and lots of memory cells, because the enemy can punch back a little.

A Note on COVID

COVID-19 vaccines included all of the classical types plus a brand-new family: mRNA vaccines. These don't inject the virus at all — they deliver instructions and your own cells briefly build the spike protein, training your immune system without ever meeting the real virus.

Cold Chain — How Vaccines Spoil

A vaccine is a fragile biological. Three things destroy it during transport:

  • Heat — proteins denature, viral particles fall apart.
  • Acidity — pH shifts ruin the antigen structure.
  • Contamination — bacteria or dirt in a cracked vial make the vaccine dangerous to use.

This is why vaccines travel in coolers with buffer solutions and arrive in sealed, single-use vials.

Katalin Karikó — The Hungarian who Saved Millions

Karikó Katalin is a Hungarian biochemist who spent decades being rejected, demoted, and almost laughed out of the field. Her obsession was making mRNA usable as medicine — every other scientist had given up because mRNA triggers immediate inflammation when injected.

Her breakthrough — chemically modifying the building blocks of mRNA so the body accepts it — became the foundation of the Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna COVID-19 vaccines. She won the 2023 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

And the part Gőz tanár úr loves to highlight: this only happened because her husband, Béla Francia, stepped fully into the role of stay-at-home parent and household manager. In an era when that was rare and quietly disapproved of, he broke gender norms so Katalin could chase a research idea everyone else thought was a dead end. Without that, no breakthrough — and most likely no COVID vaccines on the timeline we got them.
